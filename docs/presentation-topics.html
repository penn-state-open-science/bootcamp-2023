Presentation topics

The following are potential presentation topics we are considering for Day 1 and Day 2.

These are also topics that might be offered later on after the bootcamp.

Some of the presentations have been suggested by a specific person, and others are topics that Rick Gilmore thought might be appropriate for a person or group. The person indicated in the parentheses is the tentative or proposed coordinator for that workshop as of 2023-06-14. Question marks (?) indicate that the person has not yet confirmed their interest or availability. The information is merely for planning purposes. Which hands-on topics we offer will be shaped by the preferences of prospective participants, as indicated in a May 2023 survey.

0.1 Theme: What is open science? Open science @ PSU

What is open science? Why do open science? (Rick Gilmore)

Open science @ PSU

Keynote (Tim Errington)

Good enough practices for data and project management (Alaina Pearce)

Best practices can be hard to implement, so what open science practices are both ueseful and “good enough” (Wilson et al., 2017) to have an impact on your work? How can researchers make their data findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable–adhere to the FAIR data principles (Wilkinson et al., 2016)? How can these practices improve your data lifecycle and/or pipeline (e.g., data use and re-use within and between labs)?

Case study: Big data behavioral science: The Play & Learning Across a Year (PLAY) Project (Rick Gilmore)

The Play & Learning Across a Year (PLAY) Project is an NIH-funded research project that is recording, annotating, and sharing video recordings of mothers and their infants engaged in natural activities in the home. In addition to providing a first-of-its-kind openly shared video corpus of natural behavior via Databrary, PLAY’s entire protocol is openly shared on the web, and the project’s survey data results are openly summarized and regularly updated. As such, PLAY provides a test-case for how open science practices work in a large-scale, geographically distributed research project that collects and intends to share identifiable data.
